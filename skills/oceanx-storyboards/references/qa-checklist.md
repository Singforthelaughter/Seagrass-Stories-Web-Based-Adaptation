# QA Checklist

## Must pass

- Wide horizontal layout.
- Clean white background.
- Numbered navy step circles present and in correct order, each with a bold navy title.
- Teal block arrows showing the flow between panels.
- A minimalist black stick-figure actor in each panel, performing the step's core action.
- Light-blue washes used only for screens / water / AR / digital fields.
- Generous whitespace; subjects don't fill the panels.
- Restrained palette: white + black line-art + navy + teal, warm accent on at most 1–2 tiny elements.
- One coherent left-to-right journey; readable in a few seconds.
- Labels are short and legible.

## Failure signals

Regenerate or locally edit if you see:

- Detailed / photoreal / 3D figures, or figures with faces, hair, or clothing.
- Flat full-color stock-vector look, or a rainbow / heavily saturated palette.
- A literal pixel-perfect UI mockup instead of an explainer.
- Cute mascot or kawaii cartoon styling.
- Missing, duplicated, or out-of-order step numbers; garbled text.
- Arrows that aren't teal, or a palette that drifted into color fills.
- Drop shadows, bevels, textured or gradient background, paper noise.
- Crowded panels, too many props, paragraphs of text.
- The actor just standing beside the content instead of performing the action.
- Composition that copies an example storyboard too closely.

## How to iterate

- Too generic: make the actor central to the action; pick one strong prop per step.
- Too crowded: cut props and labels; one action and a few short labels per panel.
- Too cartoonish: emphasize "minimalist line-art, no face detail, not a mascot, not cute."
- Too much like a UI: replace screen detail with a simple light-blue wash and one or two icons.
- Palette drift: restate the restrained palette (white / black / navy / teal / soft light-blue, sparse warm accent); remove fills and shadows.
- Wrong/garbled numbers: prefer a local edit; if badly off, regenerate with fewer labels.
- Too much like an example: keep the meaning, change the props, staging, and actor action.

## Delivery test

A good storyboard reads as a calm, friendly walkthrough you understand in a few seconds — the numbered beats and teal arrows carry the eye, and the stick figure shows what the visitor does.

If it looks like a screenshot, a stock illustration, or a cluttered diagram instead of a clean numbered explainer strip, it fails.
